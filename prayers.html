Prayers

On this page, you will find the prayers we have covered in class with short descriptions of what the prayers are about, a transliteration of the prayer, and some key words to know. There are also videos that go over how to sing the prayers, so please feel free to recite and practice these!

Modeh Ani (מוֹדֶה אֲנִי)

Modeh Ani is the first thing we say when we get up in the morning. According to Jewish tradition, one is supposed to jump out of bed like a mighty lion to show that we are prepared for whatever challenges await us. Modeh Ani means "how grateful am I," so we start off our day by counting our blessings and thanking God for giving us the chance to wake up and have a fun day. There is no better way to start the day than saying "thank you" to God!

Homework assignment: For one week, try to list three things you are thankful for when you get up.

Transliteration (try to follow along with the video below):

Key Words:

Ani (אֲנַי)= I

Melech (מֶלֶךְ) = King

Chai (חַי) = Life

Nephesh/nishmati (נֶפֶשׁ) = Soul

Barechu (ברכו)

The Barechu is our "call to worship" prayer, which means that we are fully awake and ready to begin our service. The first part of the prayer is said by the rabbi or leader of the service, and the second part is said by the entire congregation. Barechu means both "bless" and "praise," and we are recognizing that all blessigs ultimately come from God. When you become a Bar/Bat Mitzvah, you will get to lead the congregation in this prayer when you are called to read from the Torah. This is also the prayer we say when we sing "Sweet as Honey."

Question to think about: Why do we stand and bow when we say this prayer?

Key Words:

Baruch (בָּרוּךְ): Praise/bless (most prayers in Judaism begin with "Baruch Atah Adonai")

HaMevorach (המבורך): Who is blessed

Shema (שְׁמַע)

The Shema is the most important prayer in Judaism. The word means "to listen," and in Judaism, there is a huge emphasis on listening, whether that be listening to parents, grandparents, teachers, friends, and God. In this prayer, we, the people of Israel, are recognizing that God is one, and that there is only one God. We also have the option of closing our eyes during this prayer so we can focus on saying the prayer.

Homework assignment: Sit for 5-10 minutes for one week and just listen what is around you. Really focus on listening.

Transliteration:

Key Words:

Shema (אֲנַי)= Listen/Hear

Adonai (אֲדֹנָי) = God

Echad (אֶחָד) = One

Shem (שֵׁם) = Name

Le'olam va-ed (לְעוֹלָם וָעֶד) = Forever and ever (you will hear this phrase in a lot of prayers)

Mi Chamocha (מ כמכה )

Mi Chamocha celebrates our freedom from Egypt and the successful crossing of the Red Sea. In this prayer, we are saying, "Who is like You, Adonai?" There is nothing in the entire universe that can be compared with God-- that is why we say that God is unique and special, because He cannot be compared to anything or anyone.

Homework assignment: What are some amazing things that God has done in the Bible (splitting the Red Sea, creating humans, giving us the Ten Commandments, etc.) and your life (has He blessed you with a pet or has He given you a bed to sleep in each night?)

Transliteration:

Key Words:

Kodesh (קֹדֶשׁ): Holiness (there are many variants of this word, like Kiddush and Kadosh)

Moshe/Moses (משה): The main character of the Torah

Did you catch the "le'olam va-ed" at the end?

Hashkiveinu (הַשְׁכִּיבֵנוּ)

We say Hashkiveinu right before we go to bed. Hence, we start and end our day saying  prayers. Here, we are asking God to watch over us as we sleep, to give us the best rest possible so we can wake up feeling energized and ready to do God's work in the morning.

Homework assignment: What are some amazing things that God has done in the Bible (splitting the Red Sea, creating humans, giving us the Ten Commandments, etc.) and your life (has He blessed you with a pet or has He given you a bed to sleep in each night?)

Shabbat Blessings

As Jews, we are lucky to have a holiday every week! Shabbat is a time to relax, to put away the video games and shut off the TV and hang out with friends and family. Six days a week we are busy, whether we are at school or at work, so it's nice to have an entire day to kick back and relax. We say three blessings during Shabbat: one for the wine/grape juice, the challah, and the candles.

Homework assignment: Try to turn off all electronics for one Shabbat.
